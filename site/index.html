Cetaceans

A simplified phylogeny of whales, dolphins, and porpoises

Cetacea

Cetaceans are a widely distributed and diverse clade of carnivorous, finned, aquatic marine mammals. There are around 89 species of cetaceans. Molecular evidence supports them as a relative of Artiodactyls (even-toed ungulates) which means that their closest living relatives are hippopotamuses and other hoofed mammals.[1] Some species of Cetaceans are attributed with high levels of intelligence, and are considered nonhuman persons by the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

The humpback whale, pictured here, produces a complex song lasting 10 to 20 minutes, which may have a role in mating.[2][3]

Mysticeti

Mysticeti, also know as baleen whales, are a parvorder of Cetacea which includes right whales, rorquals, the gray whale, and the pygmy right whale. Baleen whales use their baleen plates to filter out food from the water by lunge-feeding or gulp-feeding. Although widespread, most species prefer the colder waters around the Northern and Southern poles. Unlike toothed whales, baleen whales do not echolocate.[4]

The blue whale, pictured here, grows up to 100ft. and 190 tons, and is the largest animal ever known to have existed.[3]

Odontoceti

Odontoceti, also known as toothed whales, are a parvorder of Cetacea which includes sperm whales, beaked whales, dolphins, and porpoises. Toothed whales are distinguished by their use of echolocation, rather than sight, to hunt prey. Many species, mainly dolphins, are highly sociable, with some pods reaching over a thousand individuals.

The killer whale, pictured here, is the largest member of the dolphin family. Their sophisticated hunting techniques and vocalizations, which are often specific to groups and passed across generations, have been described as a form of animal culture.

Balaenopteridae

The Balaenopterids, also known as rorquals, are the largest group of baleen whales, and are distinguished by series of folds of skin running from below the mouth back to the navel, which are understood to allow the mouth to expand immensely when feeding.

At around 90ft. in length, the fin whale, pictured here, is the second largest animal on earth. It is also one of the fastest cetaceans, reaching speeds of up to 29 mph.

Balaenidae

Balaenids, also known as right whales, are large whales distinguished by their narrow, arched upper jaw, giving them a deeply curved jawline and especially long baleen plates. Right whales are robustly built compared to the relatively slender rorquals, and also lack the rorquals' distinctive throat grooves.

The bowhead whale, pictured here, has the largest mouth of any animal, and uses its massive skull to break through Arctic ice to breathe.

Phocoenidae

They are small toothed whales that are very closely related to oceanic dolphins. The most obvious visible difference between the two groups is that porpoises have shorter beaks and flattened, spade-shaped teeth distinct from the conical teeth of dolphins.

Dolphins

They are an informal grouping within the order Cetacea, excluding whales and porpoises, so to zoologists the grouping is paraphyletic. The dolphins comprise the extant families Delphinidae (the oceanic dolphins), Platanistidae (the Indian river dolphins), Iniidae (the new world river dolphins), and Pontoporiidae (the brackish dolphins).

Ziphiidae

These toothed whales are notable for their elongated beaks. Among air-breathing animals, beaked whales are some of the most extreme divers: Cuvier's beaked whales regularly dive for an hour at a depth over 1,000 m (3,300 ft),[1] and the longest and deepest foraging dive recorded is 137.5 minutes at 2,992 m (9,816 ft). This is the greatest dive-depth known for a mammal.
