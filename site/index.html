Cetaceans

A simplified phylogeny of whales, dolphins, and porpoises

Cetacea

Cetaceans are a widely distributed and diverse clade of carnivorous, finned, aquatic marine mammals. There are around 89 species of cetaceans. Molecular evidence supports them as a relative of Artiodactyls (even-toed ungulates) which means that their closest living relatives are hippopotamuses and other hoofed mammals.[1]

The humpback whale, pictured here, produces a complex song lasting 10 to 20 minutes, which may have a role in mating.[2][3]

Mysticeti

Mysticeti, also know as baleen whales, are a parvorder of Cetacea which includes right whales, rorquals, the gray whale, and the pygmy right whale. Baleen whales use their baleen plates to filter out food from the water by lunge-feeding or gulp-feeding. Although widespread, most species prefer the colder waters around the Northern and Southern poles. Unlike toothed whales, baleen whales do not echolocate.[4]

The blue whale, pictured here, grows up to 100ft. and 190 tons, and is the largest animal ever known to have existed.[3]

Balaenidae

Historically, it is known as the right whale family, as it was thought to contain only species of right whales. Through most of the 20th Century, however, that became a much-debated (and unresolved) topic amongst the scientific community.

Phocoenidae

They are small toothed whales that are very closely related to oceanic dolphins. The most obvious visible difference between the two groups is that porpoises have shorter beaks and flattened, spade-shaped teeth distinct from the conical teeth of dolphins.

Dolphins

They are an informal grouping within the order Cetacea, excluding whales and porpoises, so to zoologists the grouping is paraphyletic. The dolphins comprise the extant families Delphinidae (the oceanic dolphins), Platanistidae (the Indian river dolphins), Iniidae (the new world river dolphins), and Pontoporiidae (the brackish dolphins).

Ziphiidae

These toothed whales are notable for their elongated beaks. Among air-breathing animals, beaked whales are some of the most extreme divers: Cuvier's beaked whales regularly dive for an hour at a depth over 1,000 m (3,300 ft),[1] and the longest and deepest foraging dive recorded is 137.5 minutes at 2,992 m (9,816 ft). This is the greatest dive-depth known for a mammal.
