Cetacea

Cetaceans are a widely distributed and diverse clade of carnivorous, finned, aquatic marine mammals. There are around 89 species of cetaceans. Molecular evidence supports them as a relative of Artiodactyls (even-toed ungulates) which means that their closest living relatives are hippopotamuses and other hoofed mammals. Some species of Cetaceans are attributed with high levels of intelligence, and are considered nonhuman persons by the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

The blue whale, pictured here, grows up to 100ft. and 190 tons, and is the largest animal ever known to have existed.

Mysticeti

Mysticeti, also know as baleen whales, are a parvorder of Cetacea which includes right whales, rorquals, the gray whale, and the pygmy right whale. Baleen whales use their baleen plates to filter out food from the water by lunge-feeding or gulp-feeding. Although widespread, most species prefer the colder waters around the Northern and Southern poles. Unlike toothed whales, baleen whales do not echolocate.

The humpback whale, pictured here, produces a complex song lasting 10 to 20 minutes, which may have a role in mating.

Odontoceti

Odontoceti, also known as toothed whales, are a parvorder of Cetacea which includes sperm whales, beaked whales, dolphins, and porpoises. Toothed whales are distinguished by their use of echolocation, rather than sight, to hunt prey. Many species, mainly dolphins, are highly sociable, with some pods reaching over a thousand individuals.

The killer whale, pictured here, is the largest member of the dolphin family. Their sophisticated hunting techniques and vocalizations, which are often specific to groups and passed across generations, have been described as a form of animal culture.

Balaenopteridae

The Balaenopterids, also known as rorquals, are the largest group of baleen whales, and are distinguished by series of folds of skin running from below the mouth back to the navel, which are understood to allow the mouth to expand immensely when feeding.

At around 90ft. in length, the fin whale, pictured here, is the second largest animal on earth. It is also one of the fastest cetaceans, reaching speeds of up to 29 mph.

Balaenidae

Balaenids, also known as right whales, are large whales distinguished by their narrow, arched upper jaw, giving them a deeply curved jawline and especially long baleen plates. Right whales are robustly built compared to the relatively slender rorquals, and also lack the rorquals' distinctive throat grooves.

The bowhead whale, pictured here, has the largest mouth of any animal, and uses its massive skull to break through Arctic ice to breathe.

Phocoenidae

The Phocoenidae, also known as porpoises or mereswine, are small toothed whales that are very closely related to dolphins. Unlike dolphins, porpoises have shorter beaks and flattened, spade-shaped teeth, distinct from the conical teeth of dolphins.

The harbour porpoise, pictured here, was one of the most accessible species for early cetologists, because it could be seen very close to land.

Dolphins

Dolphins are an informal grouping within the order Cetacea, comprising several different families. Although widespread, most dolphins prefer the warmer waters of the tropics. Dolphins are highly intelligent and sociable, and exhibit culture within separate groups.

Bottlenose dolphins, pictured here, have been found to have signature whistles used to communicate with one another by identifying an individual, effectively functioning as the equivalent of a name for humans.

Cetotheriidae

Cetotheriidae was generally though to have gone extinct during the Early Pleistocene, although recent phylogenetic studies have recovered the pygmy right whale, pictured here, as a living member. In general, Cetotheriidae has been used as a wastebasket taxon for baleen whales not assignable to extant whale families.

Eschrichtiidae

The Eschrichtiidae, or gray whales, consist of a single extant species, the gray whale, pictured here. Although originally identified as a species or rorqual, skeletal differences and more recent phylogenetic studies have confirmed the placement of this species within a distinct family.

Physeteridae

Physeteridae is a family which includes three extant species; the sperm whale, pygmy sperm whale, and dwarf sperm whale. Because the eyes of physeteroids cannot swivel in their sockets, echolocation is likely a more important sense to these animals than vision.

The Sperm Whale, or cachalot, pictured here, is the largest species of toothed whale, and also has the largest brain of any animal on Earth.

Ziphiidae

The Ziphiids, or beaked whales, are notable for their elongated beaks, and are some of the most extreme divers among air-breathing animals. Because of their habitat, habits, and apparent low abundance, beaked whales are one of the least known groups of mammals.

The longest and deepest recorded foraging dive by the Cuvier's beaked whale, pictured here, lasted for 137.5 minutes at 2,992 m (9,816 ft). This is the greatest dive-depth known for a mammal.
